Theatre

Wumpus and I live in New York City and we see a lot of shows. Playbill Online has a lot of good Broadway info, and articles, too! And the OnBroadway site lets you find out not only who's in a show right now, but who got nominated for Tonys and who won!

Some of us genuflect daily in the direction of Stephen Sondheim.

Oh, Rapture!

Check out the Gilbert and Sullivan Archive! Yum, yum!

G&S enthusiasts on the net hang out at Savoynet, which is the most entertaining LISTSERV mailing list out there by far. A great community, a great bunch of people, and they even have a production of Trial by Jury under their belt!

And don't forget the Toast Point Page's Modified Rapture!, a gleaning of rehearsal variants of G&S lines and lyrics. Beware - some are quite naughty!

The Toast Point Theatrical Legacy

I spent a great deal of my college career at William and Mary involved in Sinfonicron, which is not a satanic organization, but rather a student-run light opera company. It's been around forever and boasts such luminary alumni as Calvin Remsberg. When I was a wee freshman, they did Gilbert and Sullivan every year, but during my career, they broke away and did The Merry Widow and since have produced such shows as Evita and 42nd Street.

Around the same time, I spent a couple of summers working with Wildwood Summer Theatre, a youth-run group which has also been around forever and boasts such luminary alumni as Jonathan Hadari and Stella Zambalis.

Once I got through with the school thing, I ended up in Philadelphia, where I did such killer shows as Evita and Follies with The Players Club of Swarthmore, a community theatre group whose grasp is extraordinary, and whose reach often achieves it.

Music

Eric shows you the way to wonderful artists you've never heard of:

Like all of you, I thought folk music was overly sincere hippies with bad voices singing about the rainforests - until I fell hopelessly and completely in love with Christine Lavin. And I feel oh-so-proud, because I introduced Christine's music to Susan Werner, a fellow cast member in an opera we did years ago, and Susan is now a famous (and very talented) folk singer herself and a Friend of Christine's!

I may be the only person on the east coast who's ever heard of folk/rock singer Michael Tomlinson, but I have all his albums and he's a very interesting singer. A spiritually and environmentally aware songwriter, his songs are soothing and inspiring. Check out his web page, and if you are interested in finding out what he's like, I would order Run This Way Forever as your starter album.

Another wonderful folk singer who I discovered because *blush* I liked his picture on the album cover is Maine-based David Mallett. You've probably heard his Garden Song on PBS. I would think that he'd be too close to the land to have a home page, but there it is.

Books

I read constantly (mostly mysteries, thrillers and SF, as you'll see), but I haven't found all that many web pages celebrating my favorite authors. For instance, my favorite living SF author is John Varley, but he don't have a page out there - gosh, Eric, maybe you should... nah... However, you can always start with:
  • Sue Grafton's Kinsey Millhone series. It's easy to figure out the order of this series (and I hate reading books out of order), as the first is A is for Alibi, the second is B is for Burglar and so on. She's up to N now and author Stan Cutler, in his straight/gay detective partners in Hollywood series, had his characters speculate what she might do after Z - perhaps AA is for Alcoholics Anonymous? I vote for punctuation myself.
  • Dick Francis, British author of thrillers, all in the first person, all involving horse racing in some form or other. Doesn't sound appealing? Didn't to me either at first (horses?), but once you start, you can't stop. He's been putting 'em out for thirty years and each one is a wonderful read.
  • The sadly-deceased John D. MacDonald, creater of the Florida detective and boat bum Travis McGee. I inherited my taste in trashy fiction from my mother, who had me reading Travis McGee books when I was eight. It's wonderful to reread them now that I know what the sex stuff is all about. They hold up beautifully, by the way.
  • I'm not a Florida fanatic or anything, but Florida detective fiction seems to be a new genre and I like Paul Levine's Jake Lassiter series an awful lot. Carl Hiaasen is another Floridian. His novels are hilarious and outrageous - yes, he did write Striptease, and yes, it's much better than you thought the movie was.
  • My early psyche was formed by reading lots and lots of Heinlein. He's also sadly deceased, but what a wonderful writer, and way ahead of his time. Even though, his whole last set of books seem to be about, as my uncle says, "How he wants to be a woman." Well, maybe he did!
  • Mercedes Lackey is noted for gay-friendly fantasy (no, not that kind of fantasy). I'm not particularly a sword'n'sorcery fan, but I like her books. And a good thing, because she churns out trilogy after trilogy like Lucy's chocolate factory.
I myself have written no fiction, but you can see essays I've written in Toast Points to Ponder. And of course, this is the home of The Bad Fiction Contest!
